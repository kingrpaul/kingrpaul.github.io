Holy Man of Supernatural Fiction

Algernon Blackwood was a prolific, popular, and influential writer, often associated with ‚Äúweird fiction,‚Äù but whose work spans an array of genre, subject, form, and audience.

‚ÄúThe Willows,‚Äù his best known work and Lovecraft‚Äôs favorite supernatural tale, narrates a pilgrimage by two men across central Europe‚Å†‚Äîa journey Tolkien‚Äôs readers will recognize as Frodoesque. Both Lovecraft and Tolkien acknowledged Blackwood‚Äôs influence and this influence is unmistakable any time Tolkien as author finds himself among ancient trees, climbing a mountain, or afloat on a river. For Lovecraft, it is Blackwood who transforms the ‚Äúhigher space‚Äù of Einstein‚Äôs annus mirabilis into a perilous firmament to be filled with cosmic horrors and it is Blackwood who first writes the eldritch text that is to become Lovecraft‚Äôs Necronomicon. But, while Blackwood was an explorer of the macabre corners of ‚Äúghostland;‚Äù he was captivated by the beauty of nature, believed in ‚Äútraveling light,‚Äù and never confined himself anywhere.

It had been one of Blackwood‚Äôs life goals to become a ‚Äúholy man.‚Äù He lived itinerant, unwed, and wrote only because he ‚Äúcould not keep it back.‚Äù His words were published in books, magazines, journals, newspapers; presented on stage; and broadcast on radio and television. Writing for any reader, he was published by The Country Gentleman, Lady‚Äôs Realm, and‚Å†‚Äî‚Å†for children‚Å†‚Äî‚Å†in The Merry Go Round. He wrote for both The Methodist Magazineand The Occult Review. One of his lesser-read stories was printed exclusively by The Burrowa Newsof New South Wales, in Austalia.

Even Blackwood lost count of what he had published, so that no definitive bibliography exists. Though the present collection is not exhaustive‚Å†‚Äîdoubtless some works are lost to time‚Å†‚Äîit is the most extensive of its kind. The fiction includes 16 novellas, a 3-act play, 145 short stories, 5 poems, and 3 songs. Supplementing this are 53 non-fiction works. Most provide background to the fiction stories and some are stories themselves.

While there is no ‚ÄúBlackwood Universe‚Äù in the modern sense; themes, characters, and settings are often shared. When Blackwood writes, the categories of fiction, non-fiction, and autobiography become indistinct, some works difficult to place. Of the works in this collection, twenty-one were published for children, but again the distinction is often arbitrary.

In the end, it is all Blackwood.
